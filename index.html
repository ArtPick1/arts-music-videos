Are You Living a Lie?

Confronts self-deception and surface-level faith. Inspired by 2 Corinthians 13:5.
Click to read more…
“Are You Living a Lie” presents a musically driven, lyrical reflection on spiritual identity, the emptiness of worldly pursuits, and the invitation to surrender fully to Christ. While its delivery feels intimate and declarative, it is sung throughout with expressive phrasing—blending poetic lyricism and melodic cadence.

Not Just Another Pretty Voice

A call to shout in worship—TERUAH style. Loud, raw praise that breaks down walls (Psalm 100:1–2).
Click to read more…
This song is about authentic worship that refuses to be tamed. Drawing from Psalm 100:1–2 and the meaning of “teruah”—a Hebrew word meaning a battle cry or trumpet blast—it celebrates the kind of worship that shakes spiritual walls. It challenges listeners to bring their whole selves before God without shame or performance. It’s not about sounding good — it’s about surrendering loud, messy, passionate praise to the Lord who deserves it.

The Temple of the Living God

A challenge to live holy and set apart. Based on 2 Corinthians 6:14–7:1.
Click to read more…
This song is a bold call to holiness in a compromised age. Drawing directly from 2 Corinthians 6:14–7:1, it challenges believers to be truly set apart from the world — not just in words, but in lifestyle, worship, and witness. It exposes the spiritual dangers of unequally yoked living, worldly compromise, and halfhearted faith. “The Temple of the Living God” reminds us that we are not our own — we are God’s dwelling place. The lyrics call the Church to reclaim its sacred identity and live in purity, reverence, and power. It’s a prophetic cry for the people of God to stop blending in and start standing out.

Why Go to Church?

Answers common objections and gives biblical reasons for gathering with believers (Hebrews 10:25).
Click to read more…
This song addresses one of the most common modern objections to Christian life: “Why do I even need to go to church?” It’s a heartfelt yet confrontational answer to the rise of isolation, hurt from past church experiences, and the cultural drift toward “just me and Jesus” Christianity. Rooted in Hebrews 10:25, it affirms the importance of gathering with other believers—not out of obligation, but because it’s essential for encouragement, growth, correction, worship, and spiritual warfare. The tone is compassionate but clear: the Church isn’t perfect, but God still calls us to be part of His Body — together.
